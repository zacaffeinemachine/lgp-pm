On Day 1 every experiment came with an explicit $\Omega$. Today we have already begun to drift —
"toss $n$ fair coins" and "let $\sigma$ be a uniformly random permutation" were written with no
$\Omega$ in sight. This page settles why that is allowed: the sample space is still there, we have
simply learned we rarely need to look at it.

<Lecture>

<Beat>
<Summary>

**Definition** — the distribution (or law) of $X$ is the table $t \mapsto \P(X = t)$.

</Summary>
<Detail>

> The **distribution** of a random variable $X$ with target set $T$ is the assignment
> $$ t \longmapsto \P(X = t), \qquad t \in T, $$
> recording how often $X$ takes each value.

The distribution forgets the outcome space and remembers only the values $X$ produces and how
often. We write $X \sim \mathrm{Uniform}(V)$ for "$X$ has the uniform law on $V$"; the symbol
$\sim$ is read *is distributed as*.

</Detail>
</Beat>

<Beat>
<Summary>

Two utterly different experiments can share one distribution — a coin flip and an even/odd die.

</Summary>
<Detail>

Compare $X_1$ on $\Omega_1 = \set{\texttt{H}, \texttt{T}}$ with $X_1(\texttt{H}) = 1, X_1(\texttt{T}) = 0$,
against $X_2$ on $\Omega_2 = \set{1, \ldots, 6}$ with $X_2 = 1$ on evens and $0$ on odds. The
experiments could hardly be more different, yet each variable takes $1$ with probability $\tfrac12$
and $0$ with probability $\tfrac12$ — the **same** distribution.

</Detail>
</Beat>

<Beat>
<Summary>

Expectation cannot tell the difference — it reads off only the distribution.

</Summary>
<Detail>

The value-by-value formula $\E[X] = \sum_x x\,\P(X = x)$ uses only the numbers $\P(X = x)$ — exactly
the distribution. The space $\Omega$ enters the definition $\sum_\omega X(\omega)\P(\omega)$ but
drops out of the reformulation. So $\E[X_1] = 1\cdot\tfrac12 + 0\cdot\tfrac12 = \tfrac12 = \E[X_2]$,
as the equal distributions force.

This is the licence: rather than build $\Omega$ first, we may say "**let $X$ be a variable with
such-and-such distribution**" and proceed. Behind the scenes some $(\Omega, \P)$ has been chosen; we
just never need its details when all we compute is an expectation.

</Detail>
</Beat>

<Beat>
<Summary>

A caveat, and why it will not bite today — sums need a common space, but linearity rescues us anyway.

</Summary>
<Detail>

The "$\Omega$ does not matter" argument covers one variable at a time. To form a sum
$X_1 + \cdots + X_n$ we need them on a *common* space, and one might fear $\E[X_1 + \cdots + X_n]$
depends on how they relate there. Linearity rules this out:

$$
\E[X_1 + \cdots + X_n] = \E[X_1] + \cdots + \E[X_n],
$$

and each term on the right uses one distribution alone. So whenever the question is the expectation
of a sum, the joint behaviour never enters — we trade joint bookkeeping for marginal lookups.

The escape is genuine but not unlimited. Some quantities — the **variance of a sum**, which we meet
on Day 5 — really do feel the joint distribution. Until then, as long as we compute $\E[\cdot]$, the
underlying $\Omega$ can stay out of sight.

</Detail>
</Beat>

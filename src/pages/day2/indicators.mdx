Among all random variables the simplest — and the one we will lean on most — is the **indicator**
of an event. Its job is to turn a yes/no question ("did $A$ happen?") into a $0$-or-$1$ number, so
the machinery of expectation can be aimed at it.

<Lecture>

<Beat>
<Summary>

**Definition** — the indicator $\one_A$ reads $1$ on $A$ and $0$ off it.

</Summary>
<Detail>

> For an event $A \subseteq \Omega$, the **indicator** of $A$ is the random variable
> $\one_A : \Omega \to \R$ given by
> $$ \one_A(\omega) = \begin{cases} 1 & \omega \in A, \\ 0 & \omega \notin A. \end{cases} $$

So $\one_A$ records whether $A$ occurred ($1$) or not ($0$) — nothing more.

</Detail>
</Beat>

<Beat>
<Summary>

**Key identity** — $\E[\one_A] = \P(A)$. The expectation of an indicator *is* the probability of its event.

</Summary>
<Detail>

> For any event $A$ in a finite probability space, $\E[\one_A] = \P(A)$.

The variable takes the value $1$ on $A$ and $0$ on $A^c$, so the value-by-value formula reads

$$
\E[\one_A] = 1 \cdot \P(A) + 0 \cdot \P(A^c) = \P(A).
$$

This tiny fact is the engine of nearly every calculation this week.

</Detail>
</Beat>

<Beat>
<Summary>

Two quick checks — heads of a fair coin, and an even die roll.

</Summary>
<Detail>

- **Fair coin.** On $\Omega = \set{\texttt{H}, \texttt{T}}$ with $A = \set{\texttt{H}}$, we get $\E[\one_A] = 1\cdot\tfrac12 + 0\cdot\tfrac12 = \tfrac12 = \P(A)$.
- **Even die.** On $\Omega = \set{1, \ldots, 6}$ with $A = \set{2, 4, 6}$, the indicator reads $1$ on three of six outcomes, so $\E[\one_A] = 3\cdot\tfrac16 = \tfrac12 = \P(A)$.

The identity is trivial to verify on its own. Its real power shows only once we combine it with
the next ingredient — linearity.

</Detail>
</Beat>

**Exercises**

</Summary>
<Detail>
<div class="exercises">

1. A fair die is rolled, and $A = \set{3, 6}$ is the event "the roll is a multiple of $3$". Write the value of $\one_A(\omega)$ for each outcome, compute $\E[\one_A]$ directly, and check it equals $\P(A)$.
2. Let $A$ be an event. Show that $\one_{A^c}(\omega) = 1 - \one_A(\omega)$ for every outcome $\omega \in \Omega$.
3. Let $A$ and $B$ be events. Show that $\one_{A \cap B}(\omega) = \one_A(\omega)\,\one_B(\omega)$ for every $\omega \in \Omega$.

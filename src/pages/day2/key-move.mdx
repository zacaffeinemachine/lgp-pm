The last two pages add up to a single reliable recipe. It is worth stating as a reflex, because we
will run it, with small variations, for the rest of the week.

<Lecture>

<Beat>
<Summary>

**The recipe** — write $X = \sum_i \one_{A_i}$, find each $\P(A_i)$, then $\E[X] = \sum_i \P(A_i)$.

</Summary>
<Detail>

Whenever the task is to compute $\E[X]$ for a variable that *counts* something:

1. Write $X$ as a sum of indicators $X = \sum_i \one_{A_i}$ for an explicit list of events $A_i$.
2. Compute each $\P(A_i)$ separately.
3. Apply linearity: $\E[X] = \sum_i \P(A_i)$.

The events may overlap, depend on each other, or be wildly correlated — step 3 never asks.

</Detail>
</Beat>

<Beat>
<Summary>

Worked example — the coupon collector: expected number of distinct types after $m$ days.

</Summary>
<Detail>

There are $n$ coupon types; each day one arrives, uniform among the types and independent of the
past. Let $X$ be the number of **distinct** types after $m$ days, and $A_i$ the event "type $i$ has
appeared at least once". Then $X = \one_{A_1} + \cdots + \one_{A_n}$.

Type $i$ is missed on a given day with probability $1 - \tfrac1n$, so $\P(A_i^c) = (1 - \tfrac1n)^m$
and $\P(A_i) = 1 - (1 - \tfrac1n)^m$. By linearity,

$$
\E[X] = n\left(1 - \left(1 - \tfrac1n\right)^m\right).
$$

Sanity: $m = 0$ gives $0$, and as $m \to \infty$ the answer climbs to $n$. The events share one
arrival stream and are far from independent — the recipe is unbothered.

</Detail>
</Beat>

<Beat>
<Summary>

Worked example — a shuffled deck has $12$ same-suit adjacent pairs on average.

</Summary>
<Detail>

Shuffle $52$ cards and let $X$ count the positions $i \in \set{1, \ldots, 51}$ where positions $i$
and $i+1$ share a suit. With $A_i$ that event, $X = \one_{A_1} + \cdots + \one_{A_{51}}$.

Fix the card in position $i$; of the $51$ remaining cards, exactly $12$ share its suit, and position
$i+1$ is equally likely to be any of them. So $\P(A_i) = \tfrac{12}{51}$ for every $i$, and

$$
\E[X] = 51 \cdot \tfrac{12}{51} = 12.
$$

Consecutive events share a card and so are strongly correlated — yet the manoeuvre runs on the
marginal probabilities alone.

</Detail>
</Beat>

<Beat>
<Summary>

A teaser — this same move is the engine of the probabilistic method itself.

</Summary>
<Detail>

The indicator-and-linearity move is more than a calculation aid. Tomorrow we turn it into a strategy
for *proving things exist*: build an object at random, compute one expectation, and observe that no
random quantity can sit strictly below its own average. The whole proof of our first existence
result will be a single expectation calculation.

</Detail>
</Beat>

Time to use expectation to *prove* something. The target is a basic identity of graph theory, and
the proof is a template we will reuse all week: compute one expectation in two different ways and
equate the answers.

<Lecture>

<Beat>
<Summary>

Vocabulary — a graph $G = (V, E)$, and the degree $\deg(v)$ counting the edges at a vertex.

</Summary>
<Detail>

> A **graph** $G = (V, E)$ has a finite **vertex set** $V$ and an **edge set** $E \subseteq \binom{V}{2}$
> of unordered pairs of distinct vertices. The **degree** $\deg(v)$ is the number of edges containing $v$.

Two sanity checks. In a triangle every vertex has degree $2$, so $\sum_v \deg(v) = 6 = 2 \cdot 3$,
twice the edge count. In a star with one centre and four leaves, $\sum_v \deg(v) = 4 + 4\cdot1 = 8 = 2\cdot4$.
Both land on twice the number of edges.

</Detail>
</Beat>

<Beat>
<Summary>

**Degree-sum formula** — $\sum_{v \in V} \deg(v) = 2\abs{E}$ in every finite graph.

</Summary>
<Detail>

> For a finite graph $G = (V, E)$,
> $$ \sum_{v \in V} \deg(v) = 2\abs{E}. $$

together, because adding or deleting an edge changes both by exactly $2$.

<Summary>

The proof — compute the expected degree of a random vertex two ways.

</Summary>
<Detail>

Pick a vertex $U$ uniformly at random, so $\P(U = v) = 1/\abs{V}$. **First way**, straight from the
definition of expectation:

$$
\E[\deg(U)] = \sum_{v \in V} \deg(v)\,\P(U = v) = \frac{1}{\abs{V}} \sum_{v \in V} \deg(v). \tag{$*$}
$$

**Second way.** A vertex's degree counts the edges at it, so $\deg(U) = \sum_{e \in E} \one_{\set{U \in e}}$.
Each edge has two of the $\abs{V}$ vertices, so $\P(U \in e) = 2/\abs{V}$, and by linearity

$$
\E[\deg(U)] = \sum_{e \in E} \P(U \in e) = \frac{2\abs{E}}{\abs{V}}. \tag{$**$}
$$

Equating $(*)$ and $(**)$ and multiplying by $\abs{V}$ gives $\sum_v \deg(v) = 2\abs{E}$. The same
idea — one expectation, two counts — will return for Sperner's theorem tomorrow.

</Detail>
</Beat>

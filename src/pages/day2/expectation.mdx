The expectation of a random variable is the long-run **average** value it produces over many
repetitions of the experiment. On a finite space this is exactly the weighted average of the
values, with weights given by the probabilities.

<Lecture>

<Beat>
<Summary>

**Definition** — $\E[X] = \sum_{\omega} X(\omega)\,\P(\omega)$, the average of the values weighted by their probabilities.

</Summary>
<Detail>

> Let $X$ be a **real-valued** random variable on a finite probability space $(\Omega, \P)$. Its
> **expectation** (or **mean**) is
> $$ \E[X] = \sum_{\omega \in \Omega} X(\omega)\,\P(\omega). $$

Expectation needs values we can add and weight, which is why we insist $X$ be real-valued. For a
non-numerical target set, "average" has no meaning.

</Detail>
</Beat>

<Beat>
<Summary>

The value-by-value formula $\E[X] = \sum_x x\,\P(X = x)$ is usually the convenient one.

</Summary>
<Detail>

Grouping the outcomes by the value they produce gives the equivalent form

$$
\E[X] = \sum_x x \cdot \P(X = x),
$$

where $x$ ranges over the values $X$ actually takes. The two formulas agree because the second
simply collects all outcomes with $X(\omega) = x$ into one term. Note that $\E[X]$ need not equal
any value $X$ ever takes — "average" and "typical" are different things.

</Detail>
</Beat>

<Beat>
<Summary>

Example — a fair die has $\E[X] = 3.5$, a value it never shows.

</Summary>
<Detail>

With $\Omega = \set{1, \ldots, 6}$ uniform,

$$
\E[X] = \sum_{k=1}^{6} k \cdot \tfrac16 = \frac{1 + 2 + \cdots + 6}{6} = \tfrac{21}{6} = 3.5.
$$

Roll a die many times and the running average settles on $3.5$ — even though no single roll is
ever $3.5$. Expectation is the balance point of the distribution.

<Beat>
<Summary>

Example — three coins average $1.5$ heads; a die loaded toward six averages $4.5$.

</Summary>
<Detail>

- **Three fair coins.** With the distribution $\tfrac18, \tfrac38, \tfrac38, \tfrac18$ from the previous page, $\E[X] = 0\cdot\tfrac18 + 1\cdot\tfrac38 + 2\cdot\tfrac38 + 3\cdot\tfrac18 = \tfrac{12}{8} = 1.5$.
- **A loaded die.** If $\P(6) = \tfrac12$ and $\P(k) = \tfrac{1}{10}$ for $k \in \set{1, \ldots, 5}$, then $\E[X] = (1+2+3+4+5)\cdot\tfrac{1}{10} + 6\cdot\tfrac12 = 1.5 + 3 = 4.5$. The heavy mass on $6$ pulls the mean a full unit above the fair value.

</Detail>
</Beat>

<Beat>
<Summary>

Example — a random shuffle of three labels fixes exactly $1$ label on average.

</Summary>
<Detail>

Take $\Omega = S_3$ uniform, and let $X(\sigma)$ count the **fixed points** — indices $i$ with
$\sigma(i) = i$. Listing the six orderings $\sigma(1)\sigma(2)\sigma(3)$ and their fixed-point
counts,

$$
123 \mapsto 3, \quad 132 \mapsto 1, \quad 213 \mapsto 1, \quad 231 \mapsto 0, \quad 312 \mapsto 0, \quad 321 \mapsto 1,
$$

so $\E[X] = 0\cdot\tfrac26 + 1\cdot\tfrac36 + 3\cdot\tfrac16 = 1$. Strikingly, the answer stays
exactly $1$ for shuffles of $n$ labels for *every* $n$ — and the route through linearity, two
pages on, is far shorter than this enumeration.

</Detail>
</Beat>

Before we write down a single definition, we are going to play. The graph
theory the workshop needs is easiest to meet through a puzzle, and the puzzle
stars one piece: the **knight**.

A knight jumps in an **L-shape** — two squares in one direction, then one
square at a right angle. It is the only chess piece that can hop over others,
but here we will only care about where it lands, not what it jumps over.

Below is a series of small boards. The task is to guide the knight to the
apple. Click the knight to toggle its highlighted legal moves; click any
highlighted square to jump there. Some boards have **missing squares** (literal
holes in the board), and some have **forbidden squares** marked with a skull —
the knight cannot land on those either.

Take a moment to notice a few things as we play. The knight is fussy: from some
squares it has up to eight choices, from others only two. Corners starve it.
The same destination might be one move away on a large board and impossible on
a small one. The *shape* of the board — not just the rules of the knight — is
part of the puzzle.

Keep that observation in mind. It is the first hint of an idea we will develop
carefully over the next few pages: that what matters is not the board at all,
but the pattern of *which squares connect to which*.

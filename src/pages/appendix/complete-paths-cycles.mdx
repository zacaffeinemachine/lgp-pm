A few families of graphs turn up so often that they earn standing names. We meet
three of them here: the graph with *every* edge, the simple *chain*, and the
*loop* we already stumbled onto in the knight puzzle.

<Lecture>

<Beat>
<Summary>

The **complete graph** $K_n$ has all $\binom{n}{2}$ possible edges; every vertex has degree $n - 1$.

</Summary>
<Detail>

> For an integer $n \geq 1$, the **complete graph** $K_n$ has $n$ vertices, and
> its edge set is *all* $\binom{n}{2}$ two-element subsets of $V$.

Every vertex is adjacent to every other, so each has degree $n - 1$; the
handshake lemma then confirms $\abs{E(K_n)} = \tfrac{n(n-1)}{2} = \binom{n}{2}$.

<CompleteGraphs caption="The first complete graphs. K₃ is the triangle; K₄ adds both diagonals of a square; K₅ has ten edges." />

When the workshop two-colours the edges of $K_n$ in a Ramsey argument, it is
these $\binom{n}{2}$ edges being coloured — the graph itself never changes.

</Detail>
</Beat>

<Beat>
<Summary>

A **path** $P_n$ and a **cycle** $C_n$: graphs whose vertices *can be listed* so the edges are exactly the consecutive pairs.

</Summary>
<Detail>

> Let $n \geq 1$. A graph $G = (V, E)$ is a **path** $P_n$ if $\abs{V} = n$ and
> one can list its vertices in a sequence $v_1, v_2, \ldots, v_n$ (each vertex
> appearing exactly once) so that the edges are precisely the consecutive pairs,
> and nothing else:
> $$ E = \set{v_i v_{i+1} : 1 \leq i \leq n - 1}. $$
>
> Let $n \geq 3$. A graph $G = (V, E)$ is a **cycle** $C_n$ if $\abs{V} = n$ and
> one can list its vertices as $v_1, \ldots, v_n$ (each once) so the edges are
> the consecutive pairs together with the one pair $v_n v_1$ closing the loop,
> and nothing else:
> $$ E = \set{v_i v_{i+1} : 1 \leq i \leq n - 1} \cup \set{v_n v_1}. $$

The phrase "one can list its vertices" is the heart of these definitions. The
labels $v_1, \ldots, v_n$ are not part of the graph — they are a bookkeeping
device we are free to choose. To say a graph *is* a path asserts that *some* such
listing exists; it is a structural property, not a feature of how the vertices
happen to be named. (Reading the sequence backwards gives another valid listing
of the same path.) The clause "and nothing else" forbids stray edges, so a path
has exactly its $n - 1$ chain-edges and a cycle exactly its $n$ loop-edges.

</Detail>

<Beat>
<Summary>

Degrees tell them apart: a path has two endpoints of degree $1$; a cycle is degree-$2$ everywhere.

</Summary>
<Detail>

In a path the two ends $v_1$ and $v_n$ have degree $1$ and the $n - 2$ interior
vertices have degree $2$. In a cycle *every* vertex has degree exactly $2$ — the
loop has no ends. The smallest cycle is the triangle $C_3$, which is the same
graph as $K_3$.

These two families are exactly what later questions about "triangle-free" or
"$4$-cycle-free" graphs are asking about: whether a graph contains a copy of
$C_3$, or of $C_4$, sitting inside it.

</Detail>
</Beat>

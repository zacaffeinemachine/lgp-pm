The last piece of vocabulary is what turns graphs into a playground for the
probabilistic method: we paint the edges, then ask what a *random* painting is
forced to contain.

<Lecture>

<Beat>
<Summary>

An **edge colouring** is just a function $\chi : E \to C$ assigning each edge one of a palette of colours.

</Summary>
<Detail>

> Let $G = (V, E)$ and let $C$ be a finite set of **colours**. An **edge
> colouring** of $G$ with palette $C$ is any function $\chi : E \to C$.

There is no rule here — adjacent edges *may* share a colour. (That freedom is
what distinguishes this from the "proper" colourings of other courses, which we
do not need.) A colouring is simply a choice of one colour for each edge, and
with $\abs{C}$ colours there are $\abs{C}^{\abs{E}}$ of them to choose from.

</Detail>
</Beat>

<Beat>
<Summary>

A set of vertices is **monochromatic** if it is a clique whose every edge got the same colour.

</Summary>
<Detail>

> A subset $S \subseteq V$ is **monochromatic** under $\chi$ if every two
> distinct vertices of $S$ are adjacent *and* all $\binom{\abs{S}}{2}$ edges of
> $G[S]$ receive the same colour.

So a monochromatic set is a clique painted in a single colour. Below, the edges
of $K_4$ are split into red and blue: the triangle on $\set{1,2,4}$ is
all-red — monochromatic — while the triangle on $\set{1,2,3}$ mixes colours and
is not.

</Detail>
</Beat>

<Beat>
<Summary>

This is the Ramsey setting: colour at random, and count the cliques that come out monochromatic.

</Summary>
<Detail>

The workshop's Ramsey arguments live exactly here. Take the complete graph
$K_n$, colour each of its $\binom{n}{2}$ edges by an independent coin flip, and
ask: how many $k$-vertex cliques happen to come out monochromatic? Each fixed
clique is monochromatic only if its $\binom{k}{2}$ edges all match — a rare
event — and counting the *expected* number of such cliques is what lets us prove
a colouring with **no** monochromatic $K_k$ must exist. The randomness sits on
the colouring $\chi$; the graph $K_n$ underneath stays fixed.

</Detail>
</Beat>

The knight puzzles felt strangely rigid. Four knights, nine squares, and yet
most arrangements seemed unreachable no matter how long we tried. That rigidity
is not an accident of the board — it is the *shape of the connections*, and we
can see it cleanly once we throw the board away.

## Forget the squares; keep the moves

Here is the only thing a knight cares about: from each square, which squares can
it reach in one jump? Nothing else about the board matters — not distances, not
which square looks like a corner, only this pattern of "can-jump-to".

So let us record exactly that. Draw a **dot** for each of the nine squares.
Whenever a knight can jump between two squares, join their dots with a **line**.
The dots and lines below carry every legal move, and nothing else.

Two facts jump out of the picture on the right.

First, the centre square **5 is isolated** — no line touches it. A knight
standing on the centre of a 3×3 board has nowhere to go, and no knight can ever
land there. That is why the centre seemed to "block" everything: it is not a
crossroads at all, but a dead end that play simply never visits.

Second, the eight outer squares are not a tangle. Follow the lines and they form
**one single loop**:
$$ 1 - 6 - 7 - 2 - 9 - 4 - 3 - 8 - 1. $$
Every outer square has exactly two neighbours, and the loop closes up after
visiting all eight. The knight's frantic L-shaped hops, seen through this
picture, are nothing more than steps around a circle.

## Why the swap works

Redraw that loop as an honest circle, with the four knights sitting on four of
its eight positions. A knight's move is now just a slide to an adjacent empty
spot along the circle — and here is the catch that the messy board hid from us:
**sliding pieces around a single loop can never change the order in which they
appear around it.** Pieces can travel and shuffle into the gaps, but a red that
starts clockwise-before a blue stays clockwise-before it, forever.

In the swap puzzle the two reds and two blues sit *alternately* around the loop,
evenly spaced. Swapping every red with every blue is therefore the same as
rotating the whole necklace by one notch — and a rotation is exactly the kind of
motion sliding *can* achieve. That is the real reason the colours can be
exchanged. The arrangements you found impossible were the ones that would have
required cutting the loop; the ones that worked were rotations of it in disguise.

## The object we will study

The dots-and-lines picture is the hero of this story, and it has a name: a
**graph**. The squares became its *vertices*, the legal moves became its
*edges*, and a question about a chessboard turned into a question about a loop —
a structure we named a *cycle* a moment ago without fanfare.

This is the move we will make again and again in the workshop: replace a
concrete object by the bare graph of how its parts connect, and let the shape of
that graph do the work. The remaining pages of this appendix pin down the
vocabulary precisely — vertices and edges, degrees, paths and cycles, and a few
more — so that when these words appear in the lectures, they come with no
surprises.

Two simple operations let us build new graphs out of old ones, and both are
workhorses in the arguments to come: taking *part* of a graph, and *flipping*
which pairs are joined.

<Lecture>

<Beat>
<Summary>

A **subgraph** keeps some vertices and some of their edges; an **induced** subgraph $G[V']$ keeps *all* edges among the chosen vertices.

</Summary>
<Detail>

> A graph $H = (V', E')$ is a **subgraph** of $G = (V, E)$, written
> $H \subseteq G$, if $V' \subseteq V$ and $E' \subseteq E$ (with both endpoints
> of every edge in $E'$ lying in $V'$).
>
> The subgraph is **induced** if $E'$ contains *every* edge of $G$ with both
> endpoints in $V'$. We then write $H = G[V']$, the subgraph **induced** by $V'$.

The difference is whether we are *free* to drop edges or *forced* to keep them.
A plain subgraph on $\set{a,b,c}$ may leave out an edge that $G$ has; the induced
subgraph $G[\set{a,b,c}]$ must include it.

<SubgraphInduced caption="Both graphs use the vertices {a, b, c}. Left: a subgraph that omits edge bc. Right: the induced subgraph G[{a, b, c}], which is forced to keep bc because G has it." />

A subgraph that keeps *all* the vertices, $V' = V$, but is free to drop edges, is
called a **spanning** subgraph. The half-the-edges bipartite subgraph the
workshop builds later is spanning in exactly this sense.

</Detail>
</Beat>

<Beat>
<Summary>

The **complement** $\bar G$ joins exactly the pairs that $G$ leaves apart.

</Summary>
<Detail>

> The **complement** of $G = (V, E)$ is the graph $\bar G = (V, \bar E)$ on the
> same vertices, where $\set{u, v} \in \bar E$ precisely when $u \neq v$ and
> $\set{u, v} \notin E$. In words: $u$ and $v$ are adjacent in $\bar G$ if and
> only if they are *not* adjacent in $G$.

Together, $G$ and $\bar G$ split the full edge set of $K_n$ between them: every
pair of distinct vertices is an edge of exactly one of the two. So
$\abs{E(G)} + \abs{E(\bar G)} = \binom{n}{2}$.

<Complement caption="A graph G (its edges in black) and its complement (red). Here G is the 5-cycle and its complement is the 'pentagram' — together they fill in all ten edges of K₅." />

Flipping to the complement is a quiet but powerful trick: it will let the
workshop trade a statement about *dense* structure in $G$ for one about *sparse*
structure in $\bar G$, and read one theorem off another.

</Detail>
</Beat>

The knight puzzle taught us the idea; now we name it. A graph records
relationships among a fixed collection of objects: which pairs are tied together
by some chosen relation, and which are not. Drawn on paper, the objects become
dots and the ties become lines — but stripped of the picture, the underlying
structure is just two sets.

<Lecture>

<Beat>
<Summary>

A **graph** is a pair $G = (V, E)$: a set $V$ of **vertices** and a set $E$ of **edges**, each edge a pair of vertices.

</Summary>
<Detail>

> A **graph** $G$ is a pair $(V, E)$ where $V$ is a finite non-empty set of
> **vertices**, and $E$ is a set of two-element subsets of $V$, called
> **edges**. If $\set{u, v} \in E$ we say $u$ and $v$ are **adjacent** (or
> **neighbours**), and the edge $\set{u, v}$ is **incident** to each of $u$ and $v$.

The picture is a convenience, not part of the data: any two drawings of the same
$(V, E)$ represent the same graph. Below is one drawing of a graph on five
vertices.

<Beat>
<Summary>

Our graphs are **simple** and **undirected** — no directions, no loops, no repeated edges.

</Summary>
<Detail>

Three features of the definition deserve emphasis.

- An edge is an *unordered* pair: $\set{u, v}$ is the same edge as $\set{v, u}$.
  There is no preferred direction — all our graphs are **undirected**.
- An edge is a subset of size *exactly two*. So $\set{u, u}$ is not allowed (no
  **loops**), and no two distinct edges share the same pair of endpoints (no
  **parallel edges**). Graphs obeying these restrictions are called **simple**;
  in this workshop "graph" always means "simple graph".
- $V$ and $E$ are abstract sets. Sliding the dots around the page, or bending
  the lines, changes the drawing but not the graph — and not a single theorem we
  prove about it.

</Detail>
</Beat>

<Beat>
<Summary>

**Order** $\abs{V}$, **size** $\abs{E}$, and the shorthands $n, m, uv, u \sim v$.

</Summary>
<Detail>

The number of vertices $\abs{V}$ is the **order** of $G$; the number of edges
$\abs{E}$ is its **size**. When several graphs are in play we write $V(G)$ and
$E(G)$. A few conventions we use throughout:

- $n = \abs{V}$ and $m = \abs{E}$ when no confusion arises.
- The edge $\set{u, v}$ is often abbreviated $uv$, with $uv$ and $vu$ naming the
  same edge.
- $u \sim v$ is shorthand for "$u$ and $v$ are adjacent", i.e. $\set{u, v} \in E$.

None of this is deep — it is the vocabulary one picks up in a single sitting. We
collect it here only so that nothing in the lectures comes as a surprise.

</Detail>

Some graphs have a two-sided character: their vertices fall into two camps, and
every edge runs between the camps rather than inside one. This shape is common
enough — and useful enough in the workshop — to deserve its own name.

<Lecture>

<Beat>
<Summary>

A graph is **bipartite** if its vertices split as $V = A \sqcup B$ with every edge crossing from $A$ to $B$.

</Summary>
<Detail>

> A graph $G = (V, E)$ is **bipartite** if there is a partition $V = A \sqcup B$
> such that every edge has one endpoint in $A$ and the other in $B$. The pair
> $(A, B)$ is a **bipartition** of $G$.

No edge is allowed to live entirely inside $A$ or entirely inside $B$. Think of
$A$ and $B$ as two guest lists where ties only ever cross the aisle.

<Bipartite caption="Left: a bipartite graph with parts A and B — every edge crosses between them. Right: the complete bipartite graph K₃,₃, which has all 3 × 3 = 9 crossing edges." />

</Detail>
</Beat>

<Beat>
<Summary>

The **complete bipartite** graph $K_{m,n}$ has all $mn$ crossing edges.

</Summary>
<Detail>

> For positive integers $m, n$, the **complete bipartite graph** $K_{m,n}$ has
> vertex set $A \sqcup B$ with $\abs{A} = m$ and $\abs{B} = n$, and its edges are
> *all* $mn$ pairs $\set{a, b}$ with $a \in A$ and $b \in B$.

So $K_{m,n}$ is the densest bipartite graph on those two parts: nothing inside a
part, everything across. A small but useful identity — $K_{2,2}$ is the same
graph as the four-cycle $C_4$. (Draw two dots opposite two dots, join all four
crossing pairs, and the square loop appears.)

</Detail>
</Beat>

<Beat>
<Summary>

The test: a graph is bipartite **exactly when it has no odd cycle**.

</Summary>
<Detail>

Why can some graphs *not* be split this way? Trace a cycle while trying to
two-colour the vertices $A$/$B$ so neighbours differ: the colours must alternate
$A, B, A, B, \ldots$ around it. That only closes up consistently if the cycle
has *even* length. An odd cycle — a triangle, a pentagon — forces a clash.

This gives a clean criterion: **a graph is bipartite if and only if it contains
no odd cycle.** In particular $K_n$ is bipartite only for $n \leq 2$; from
$n = 3$ on it contains the triangle $C_3$ and cannot be split. Bipartite graphs
return later when the workshop shows that any graph has a bipartite subgraph
carrying at least half of its edges.

</Detail>
</Beat>

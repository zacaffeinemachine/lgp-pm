Among all the subsets of vertices, two kinds sit at opposite extremes: those
where *everyone* is joined, and those where *no one* is. Both are central to the
extremal results the workshop proves.

<Lecture>

<Beat>
<Summary>

A **clique** is a set of mutually adjacent vertices; an **independent set** has no edges inside it at all.

</Summary>
<Detail>

> A subset $S \subseteq V$ is a **clique** if every two distinct vertices of $S$
> are adjacent — that is, $G[S]$ is a complete graph $K_{\abs{S}}$.
>
> A subset $I \subseteq V$ is an **independent set** if no two vertices of $I$
> are adjacent — that is, $G[I]$ has no edges.

A clique is a fully-connected clump; an independent set is a scattering of
mutual strangers. They are perfect opposites, and the same vertices can play
very different roles depending on the graph.

</Detail>
</Beat>

<Beat>
<Summary>

The largest sizes get names: the **clique number** $\omega(G)$ and the **independence number** $\alpha(G)$.

</Summary>
<Detail>

> The **clique number** $\omega(G)$ is the size of the largest clique in $G$;
> the **independence number** $\alpha(G)$ is the size of the largest independent
> set in $G$.

For the graph above, the triangle gives $\omega(G) = 3$, and no two non-adjacent
vertices extend to a non-adjacent triple, so $\alpha(G) = 2$. These two numbers
measure, respectively, the densest and the emptiest patch a graph contains.

</Detail>
</Beat>

<Beat>
<Summary>

They are mirror images through the complement: $\omega(G) = \alpha(\bar G)$ and $\alpha(G) = \omega(\bar G)$.

</Summary>
<Detail>

Here is where the complement from the previous page pays off. A set $S$ is a
*clique* of $G$ exactly when every pair inside it is an edge of $G$ — which is
exactly when *no* pair inside it is an edge of $\bar G$, i.e. $S$ is an
*independent set* of $\bar G$. The roles swap perfectly:
$$ \omega(G) = \alpha(\bar G), \qquad \alpha(G) = \omega(\bar G). $$

This duality is the hinge of the workshop's argument for Turán's theorem: a
guaranteed *large independent set* in one graph becomes a statement about
*avoiding large cliques* in its complement. One picture, read from both sides.

</Detail>
</Beat>

When every outcome is equally likely, the measure is forced — and probability reduces to
counting.

<Lecture>

<Beat>
<Summary>

Equally likely outcomes: $\P(A) = \abs{A} / \abs{\Omega}$.

</Summary>
<Detail>

If $\Omega$ is finite and each outcome is equally likely, every singleton gets
probability $1/\abs{\Omega}$, and by (P3)

$$
\P(A) = \frac{\abs{A}}{\abs{\Omega}} = \frac{\#\set{\text{favourable outcomes}}}{\#\set{\text{total outcomes}}}.
$$

This is the **uniform probability measure**. Almost every Day 1 problem uses it.

</Detail>
</Beat>

<Beat>
<Summary>

Example — two dice: $\P(\text{sum}=7) = 6/36$; and an urn of $5$ red, $3$ blue: $\P(\text{both red}) = 5/14$.

</Summary>
<Detail>

**Two dice.** $\Omega = \set{1,\dots,6}^2$, $\abs{\Omega} = 36$. The event $a+b = 7$ has
six outcomes, so $\P = 6/36 = 1/6$.

**An urn.** $5$ red and $3$ blue balls, draw two without replacement; both red? With
$\Omega$ the unordered pairs, $\abs{\Omega} = \binom{8}{2} = 28$ and the red pairs number
$\binom{5}{2} = 10$, giving $10/28 = 5/14$. Counting ordered pairs gives
$\tfrac{5\cdot4}{8\cdot7} = \tfrac{5}{14}$ — the same, as it must.

</Detail>
</Beat>

<Beat>
<Summary>

Warm-up — a $3$-card deck has $3! = 6$ orderings; write them out and read off $\P(\text{Ace on top}) = 1/3$.

</Summary>
<Detail>

**A three-card deck, by hand.** Before trusting factorials, watch "every ordering equally
likely" on a deck small enough to write out. Shuffle an Ace, a King, and a Queen — the
sample space is all $3! = 6$ orderings,

$$
\mathrm{AKQ},\quad \mathrm{AQK},\quad \mathrm{KAQ},\quad \mathrm{KQA},\quad \mathrm{QAK},\quad \mathrm{QKA},
$$

each of probability $1/6$.

*Ace on top?* Two orderings ($\mathrm{AKQ}, \mathrm{AQK}$) start with the Ace, so
$\P = 2/6 = 1/3$ — the $1/n$ that becomes $1/52$ on a full deck. *Ace before the King?*
Three of the six ($\mathrm{AKQ}, \mathrm{AQK}, \mathrm{QAK}$), so $\P = 1/2$: a fair shuffle
has no reason to prefer one order of the two to the other.

</Detail>
</Beat>

<Beat>
<Summary>

A shuffled deck — $\Omega = S_{52}$, so $\P(\text{top card is } A\spadesuit) = 1/52$ and $\P(\text{top two are aces}) = 1/221$.

</Summary>
<Detail>

**A shuffled deck.** Shuffle $52$ cards so every ordering is equally likely: $\Omega$ is
the set of all orderings, $\abs{\Omega} = 52!$. Most questions become a permutation count.

*Top card the Ace of Spades?* Fix $A\spadesuit$ on top and arrange the other $51$ cards
below it in $51!$ ways:

$$
\P(\text{top card is } A\spadesuit) = \frac{51!}{52!} = \frac{1}{52}.
$$

Nothing favours that card or that slot — **any** named card is equally likely in **any**
named position. *Top two cards both aces?* There are $4 \cdot 3$ ways to seat an ordered
pair of aces up top and $50!$ ways to fill the rest:

$$
\P(\text{top two are aces}) = \frac{4 \cdot 3 \cdot 50!}{52!} = \frac{12}{52 \cdot 51} = \frac{1}{221}.
$$

</Detail>
</Beat>

<Beat>
<Summary>

The birthday problem — $30$ students: count the complement "all distinct" to get $\P(\text{some share}) \approx 0.706$.

</Summary>
<Detail>

A class of $30$ students, each birthday equally likely among $365$ days, chosen
independently — probability that **some two share** a birthday? Take
$\Omega = \set{1,\dots,365}^{30}$, so $\abs{\Omega} = 365^{30}$. Count the **complement**,
"all distinct", as ordered tuples of distinct days:

$$
365 \cdot 364 \cdots 336 = \frac{365!}{335!}.
$$

Then $\P(\text{all distinct}) = \dfrac{365 \cdot 364 \cdots 336}{365^{30}} \approx 0.294$,
so a shared birthday has probability about $0.706$ — better than two in three.

<BirthdayProblem client:visible />

The lesson, recurring all week: with $30$ items in $365$ slots, **collisions are far
more likely** than the size of the room suggests.

</Detail>

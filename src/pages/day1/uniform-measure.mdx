When every outcome is equally likely, the measure is forced — and probability reduces to
counting.

<Lecture>

<Beat>
<Summary>

Equally likely outcomes: $\P(A) = \abs{A} / \abs{\Omega}$.

</Summary>
<Detail>

If $\Omega$ is finite and each outcome is equally likely, every singleton gets
probability $1/\abs{\Omega}$, and by (P3)

$$
\P(A) = \frac{\abs{A}}{\abs{\Omega}} = \frac{\#\set{\text{favourable outcomes}}}{\#\set{\text{total outcomes}}}.
$$

This is the **uniform probability measure**. Almost every Day 1 problem uses it.

</Detail>
</Beat>

<Beat>
<Summary>

Example — two dice, and an urn.

</Summary>
<Detail>

**Two dice.** $\Omega = \set{1,\dots,6}^2$, $\abs{\Omega} = 36$. The event $a+b = 7$ has
six outcomes, so $\P = 6/36 = 1/6$.

**An urn.** $5$ red and $3$ blue balls, draw two without replacement; both red? With
$\Omega$ the unordered pairs, $\abs{\Omega} = \binom{8}{2} = 28$ and the red pairs number
$\binom{5}{2} = 10$, giving $10/28 = 5/14$. Counting ordered pairs gives
$\tfrac{5\cdot4}{8\cdot7} = \tfrac{5}{14}$ — the same, as it must.

</Detail>
</Beat>

<Beat>
<Summary>

The birthday problem — collisions are likelier than you think.

</Summary>
<Detail>

A class of $30$ students, each birthday equally likely among $365$ days, chosen
independently — probability that **some two share** a birthday? Take
$\Omega = \set{1,\dots,365}^{30}$, so $\abs{\Omega} = 365^{30}$. Count the **complement**,
"all distinct", as ordered tuples of distinct days:

$$
365 \cdot 364 \cdots 336 = \frac{365!}{335!}.
$$

Then $\P(\text{all distinct}) = \dfrac{365 \cdot 364 \cdots 336}{365^{30}} \approx 0.294$,
so a shared birthday has probability about $0.706$ — better than two in three.

The lesson, recurring all week: with $30$ items in $365$ slots, **collisions are far
more likely** than the size of the room suggests.

</Detail>
</Beat>

## Exercises

1. Roll a fair die twice. Find $\P(\text{the sum is } 5)$ and $\P(\text{the sum is even})$.
2. A box holds $4$ red and $2$ green balls; draw two without replacement. Find $\P(\text{both are red})$.
3. A number is chosen uniformly at random from $\set{1, 2, \dots, 10}$. Find $\P(\text{it is prime})$.

The workshop is about a powerful proof technique — the **probabilistic method** — but
it rests on a small handful of ideas we need to make precise. Before any definitions,
let us see what goes wrong when we don't. Start by playing a game.

## A game against the house

We flip a fair coin over and over, writing `H` for heads and `T` for tails, until a run
of **three flips in a row** matches somebody's bet. You pick a sequence of three; the
house picks one too. We keep flipping until one of the two patterns appears as three
*consecutive* flips — whoever's pattern shows up first wins the round. Pick your bet,
choose how many rounds to watch and how fast, and press **Watch**.

A fair coin, sequences of equal length — surely it is an even contest? Hold on to
whatever the simulation just showed you. That flicker of surprise is exactly the
feeling this first day is about: confident intuition, gently defeated by careful
counting.

## A coin, tossed twice

Here is the same lesson in miniature. Two students are asked: *a fair coin is tossed
twice; what is the chance that both tosses show heads?*

- The **first** reasons: "There are three outcomes — two heads, two tails, or one of
  each — so the answer is $1/3$."
- The **second** insists: "There are four equally likely outcomes — `HH`, `HT`, `TH`,
  `TT` — so the answer is $1/4$."

Both sound plausible, and both cannot be right. Yet neither has done anything obviously
foolish: each listed "the possible outcomes" and counted the favourable ones. The
disagreement is not about arithmetic. It is about **what experiment is being
performed**, and which list of outcomes is the honest one.

Once we record the two tosses *in order* — so `HH`, `HT`, `TH`, `TT` is the right list
and each is equally likely — the answer is forced: $1/4$. The first student folded two
genuinely different outcomes (first-head-then-tail, and its reverse) into one entry
while still pretending each entry was equally likely. The phrase "the probability of
$X$" is only half a sentence: it is finished only when we say **which experiment** $X$
refers to and **how likelihoods are assigned**.

## "At random" makes it worse

The trouble grows the moment the words *at random* appear. Pick a chord of a circle "at
random"; what is the probability it is longer than the side of the inscribed
equilateral triangle? Three perfectly reasonable readings give three different answers.

- Pick the two **endpoints** uniformly and independently on the circle → $1/3$.
- Pick a **radius**, then a point on it uniformly, and take the chord perpendicular
  there → $1/2$.
- Pick the **midpoint** of the chord uniformly from the disc → $1/4$.

None is *wrong*. They are three different experiments wearing the same English costume;
the question has no answer until the questioner says *which* random chord they mean.

## The escape is discipline

The way out is not cleverness; it is discipline. Before we calculate anything, we
commit — in writing — to two ingredients:

> A set $\Omega$ listing all conceivable outcomes of the experiment, and a rule $\P$
> that assigns to each subset of $\Omega$ a number in $[0,1]$ representing how likely
> the outcome is to lie in that subset.

Once these are fixed, every probability becomes a well-defined number, and a
disagreement reduces to an honest question: which $\Omega$ and which $\P$ model the
experiment? The rest of Day 1 makes this precise — starting with the language of sample
spaces and events.

The workshop is about a powerful proof technique — the **probabilistic method** — but
the technique rests on a small handful of ideas we need to make precise first. Before
we write down any definitions, let us see what goes wrong when we don't.

## A coin, tossed twice

Two students are asked: *a fair coin is tossed twice; what is the chance that both
tosses show heads?*

- The **first** reasons: "There are three outcomes — two heads, two tails, or one of
  each — so the answer is $1/3$."
- The **second** insists: "There are four equally likely outcomes — `HH`, `HT`, `TH`,
  `TT` — so the answer is $1/4$."

Both sound plausible read quickly, and both cannot be right. Yet neither has done
anything obviously foolish: each listed "the possible outcomes" and counted the
favourable ones. The disagreement is not about arithmetic. It is about **what
experiment is being performed**, and which list of outcomes is the honest one.

Once we agree to record the two tosses *in order* — so `HH`, `HT`, `TH`, `TT` is the
right list and each is equally likely — the answer is forced: $1/4$. The first
student's list wasn't wrong arithmetically; it was wrong because it folded two
genuinely different outcomes (first-head-then-tail, and its reverse) into one entry
while still pretending each entry was equally likely.

This small disagreement is the whole story in miniature. The phrase "the probability
of $X$" is only half a sentence. It is completed only when we also commit to **which
experiment** $X$ refers to and **how likelihoods are assigned** to its outcomes. Change
either ingredient and the same English words can yield a different number.

## "At random" makes it worse

The trouble grows the moment the words *at random* appear. Here is a beautiful old
problem: pick a chord of a circle "at random"; what is the probability that it is
longer than the side of the inscribed equilateral triangle? Three perfectly reasonable
readings of "random chord" give three different answers.

- Pick the two **endpoints** uniformly and independently on the circle → probability $1/3$.
- Pick a **radius**, then a point on it uniformly, and take the chord perpendicular
  there → probability $1/2$.
- Pick the **midpoint** of the chord uniformly from the disc → probability $1/4$.

None of the three is *wrong*. They are three different experiments wearing the same
English costume. The question has no answer until the questioner says *which* random
chord they mean. Even "pick a student at random from a class of thirty" hides the same
trap: drawing a roll number from a bowl, taking the first to arrive tomorrow, or
weighting by some unstated criterion are different procedures that place different
probabilities on the same event.

## The escape is discipline

The way out is not cleverness; it is discipline. Before we calculate anything, we
commit — in writing — to two ingredients:

> A set $\Omega$ listing all conceivable outcomes of the experiment, and a rule $\P$
> that assigns to each subset of $\Omega$ a number in $[0,1]$ representing how likely
> the outcome is to lie in that subset.

Once these are fixed, every probability becomes a well-defined number, and a
disagreement like our two students' reduces to an honest question: which $\Omega$ and
which $\P$ correctly model the experiment? The rest of Day 1 makes this discipline
precise, starting with the language of sample spaces and events.

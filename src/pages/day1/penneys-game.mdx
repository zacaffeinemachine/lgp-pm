Here is a game you can play against the house.

We are going to flip a fair coin over and over, writing down `H` for heads
and `T` for tails, until a run of **three flips in a row** matches somebody's
bet. Each of us picks a sequence of three in advance — say you bet on `HHT`
and the house bets on `THH`. We then flip until one of those two patterns
appears as three *consecutive* flips. Whoever's pattern shows up first wins
the round.

It feels like a coin toss between equals: the coin is fair, and a sequence of
three heads-or-tails looks as likely as any other. So surely picking first,
or picking second, makes no difference?

**Pick your bet below and find out.** In *House auto-beats you* mode, the house
waits to see your choice and then picks its own. Play a hundred rounds, then a
thousand, then ten thousand, and watch the win column.

You will notice something strange: no matter which sequence you choose, the
house seems to come out ahead — often by a lot. This is **Penney's game**, and
it is our first example of a phenomenon that runs through this whole workshop:
careful probability can defeat confident intuition.

## What is going on?

The catch is that runs of coin flips do **not** behave like independent prizes
in a lottery. Some patterns "wait for" others. Given your sequence
$s_1 s_2 s_3$, the house picks

$$
\overline{s_2}\, s_1 s_2,
$$

that is: the *opposite* of your second symbol, followed by your first and second
symbols. If you bet `HHH`, the house bets `THH` and wins **seven rounds out of
eight**. The reason, in a sentence: before your three-in-a-row can finish, the
house's pattern has usually already sneaked in just before it. We will make
this precise once we have the language of sample spaces and events.

<details>
<summary>One more surprise: the game is not transitive.</summary>

Switch to **Free play** and try a little tournament. Sequence `A` can beat `B`,
and `B` can beat `C`, and yet `C` beats `A`. There is no single "best" sequence
— for every choice, some other choice beats it. (Compare this to the
*Watch one round* button: it shows you a single game flip by flip, so you can
see exactly where one pattern overtakes the other.) Non-transitivity like this
will come back when we study tournaments and random structures later in the
week.

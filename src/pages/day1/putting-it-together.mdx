Every problem we solve this week — elementary or advanced — has the same shape:

> 1. **Choose a sample space** $\Omega$ that captures the experiment.
> 2. **Choose a probability measure** $\P$ on $\Omega$ (most often, the uniform measure on a cleverly chosen $\Omega$).
> 3. **Identify the event** $A$ whose probability you want.
> 4. **Compute** $\P(A)$, by counting or by combining simpler probabilities using the rules.

The **art** lies in steps (1) and (2): the right $\Omega$ can turn an unwieldy question
into a one-line calculation. Step (4) is then mostly a careful count.

Tomorrow we add the single most useful tool built on top of this language — the
**expectation** of a random variable — and the surprisingly powerful principle that its
behaviour is *linear*.

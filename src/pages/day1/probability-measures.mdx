A probability measure assigns a **weight** to each individual outcome, recording how
likely it is. The probability of an **event** is then just the total weight inside it.

> **Definition.** Let $\Omega$ be a finite sample space. A **probability measure** is a
> function $\P : \Omega \to [0,1]$ whose values on the outcomes sum to $1$:
> $$\sum_{\omega \in \Omega} \P(\omega) = 1.$$
> The number $\P(\omega)$ is the **probability mass** of the outcome $\omega$.

With masses in hand, the probability of any event $A \subseteq \Omega$ is the sum of
the masses of its outcomes:

$$
\P(A) := \sum_{\omega \in A} \P(\omega).
$$

(We write $\P$ both for the function on outcomes and the one on events; context makes
clear which is meant.) Three consequences come up so often we name them.

> **(P1) Total probability is $1$:** $\P(\Omega) = \sum_{\omega}\P(\omega) = 1$ — something always happens.
>
> **(P2) Probabilities are non-negative:** $\P(A) = \sum_{\omega \in A}\P(\omega) \ge 0$.
>
> **(P3) Disjoint events add:** if $A \cap B = \emptyset$, then $\P(A \cup B) = \P(A) + \P(B)$; and more generally $\P(A_1 \cup \cdots \cup A_n) = \P(A_1) + \cdots + \P(A_n)$ for pairwise-disjoint events.

Almost every calculation rests on (P1) and (P3).

## What does $\P(A)$ *mean*?

The definition tells us how to *manipulate* probabilities, but not what the number
*represents*. In what sense is $1/6$ the "right" probability of rolling a $3$?

The most popular answer is the **frequentist interpretation**. Imagine repeating the
experiment under identical conditions $N$ times and recording, for each event $A$, the
fraction

$$
\hat{p}_N(A) = \frac{\#\set{\text{trials in which } A \text{ occurred}}}{N}.
$$

The interpretation says $\P(A)$ is the long-run value of $\hat{p}_N(A)$ as $N$ grows.
Toss a fair coin a thousand times and you see roughly $500$ heads; a million times,
roughly $500{,}000$. The fraction stabilises near $1/2$, and we declare
$\P(\text{heads}) = 1/2$.

{/* WIDGET: frequentist convergence — toss a coin / roll a die N times, plot the running fraction approaching P(A) */}

Read through this lens, (P1)–(P3) become *inevitable* rather than imposed: empirical
fractions over all of $\Omega$ sum to $1$, are non-negative, and add over disjoint
events exactly. A caution, though: this tells us what $\P(A)$ *means*, not how to
*compute* it — we never run an experiment infinitely often. In practice we **model**
the experiment (often by symmetry), write down $\Omega$ and $\P$, and compute. The
frequentist picture is the bridge between model and reality. (That
$\hat{p}_N(A) \to \P(A)$ is itself a theorem — the **law of large numbers** — whose
quantitative cousin, Chebyshev's inequality, we meet on Day 5.)

## Examples

{/* WIDGET: fair/biased coin builder — drag P(H), watch the masses sum to 1 and event probabilities update */}

- **Fair coin.** $\Omega = \set{\texttt{H},\texttt{T}}$, $\P(\texttt{H}) = \P(\texttt{T}) = 1/2$. Masses sum to $1$.
- **Biased coin.** Same $\Omega$, but $\P(\texttt{H}) = 2/3$, $\P(\texttt{T}) = 1/3$. Still sums to $1$ — so $\P$ is its own ingredient, separate from $\Omega$: one sample space carries infinitely many measures.
- **Fair die.** $\P(k) = 1/6$ for each $k$. For $A = \set{2,4,6}$, $\P(A) = 3 \cdot \tfrac16 = \tfrac12$.
- **Loaded die.** Same $\Omega$, but $\P(6) = 1/2$ and each other face $1/10$ (sum $= 5\cdot\tfrac1{10} + \tfrac12 = 1$). Now the *same* event $\set{2,4,6}$ has $\P(A) = \tfrac1{10}+\tfrac1{10}+\tfrac12 = \tfrac{7}{10}$. Same event, same $\Omega$ — different measure, different answer.
- **Degenerate measure.** On $\Omega = \set{a,b,c}$, taking $\P(a)=1$, $\P(b)=\P(c)=0$ is legal but boring: $\set{b,c}$ has probability $0$.

## Consequences of the rules

A few simple consequences get used constantly.

> **Elementary properties.** For events $A, B \subseteq \Omega$:
> 1. $\P(\emptyset) = 0$;
> 2. $\P(A^c) = 1 - \P(A)$;
> 3. if $A \subseteq B$, then $\P(A) \le \P(B)$;
> 4. $\P(A \cup B) = \P(A) + \P(B) - \P(A \cap B)$;
> 5. $\P(A \cup B) \le \P(A) + \P(B)$ — the **union bound**.

*Why:* (1) $\emptyset$ is disjoint from itself, so $\P(\emptyset) = 2\P(\emptyset)$,
forcing $0$. (2) $A$ and $A^c$ are disjoint with union $\Omega$, so their probabilities
sum to $1$. (3) Write $B = A \cup (B \setminus A)$ as a disjoint union. (4) Split both
$A \cup B$ and $B$ into disjoint pieces and add. (5) Follows from (4) since
$\P(A \cap B) \ge 0$.

The union bound extends to any number of events,
$\P(A_1 \cup \cdots \cup A_n) \le \P(A_1) + \cdots + \P(A_n)$, and — despite its
simplicity — will be one of the workhorses of the workshop.

A probability measure assigns a **weight** to each outcome; the probability of an event
is the total weight inside it.

<Lecture>

<Beat>
<Summary>

**Definition** — a probability measure: masses on outcomes that sum to $1$.

</Summary>
<Detail>

> Let $\Omega$ be a finite sample space. A **probability measure** is a function
> $\P : \Omega \to [0,1]$ with $\sum_{\omega \in \Omega} \P(\omega) = 1$. The number
> $\P(\omega)$ is the **probability mass** of the outcome $\omega$.

The probability of an event $A \subseteq \Omega$ is the sum of its outcomes' masses:

$$
\P(A) := \sum_{\omega \in A} \P(\omega).
$$

</Detail>
</Beat>

<Beat>
<Summary>

Three properties: total is $1$, non-negative, disjoint events add.

</Summary>
<Detail>

> **(P1)** $\P(\Omega) = 1$ — something always happens.
> **(P2)** $\P(A) \ge 0$ for every event.
> **(P3)** if $A \cap B = \emptyset$ then $\P(A \cup B) = \P(A) + \P(B)$; more generally,
> disjoint events $A_1, \dots, A_n$ satisfy $\P(A_1 \cup \cdots \cup A_n) = \P(A_1) + \cdots + \P(A_n)$.

Almost every calculation rests on (P1) and (P3).

</Detail>
</Beat>

<Beat>
<Summary>

What $\P(A)$ *means* — the long-run fraction $\hat p_N(A) \to \P(A)$, which makes the three rules inevitable. (We compute via a model, not infinite trials.)

</Summary>
<Detail>

Repeat the experiment $N$ times and record the fraction
$\hat{p}_N(A) = \#\set{\text{trials where } A \text{ occurred}} / N$. The frequentist
interpretation says $\P(A)$ is the long-run value of $\hat{p}_N(A)$ as $N$ grows. Toss a
fair coin a thousand times and you see roughly $500$ heads; the fraction stabilises near
$1/2$, so $\P(\text{heads}) = 1/2$. Through this lens (P1)–(P3) become inevitable.

A caution: this says what $\P(A)$ *means*, not how to *compute* it — we never run an
experiment infinitely often. In practice we **model** the experiment (often by
symmetry), write down $\Omega$ and $\P$, and compute. (That $\hat{p}_N(A) \to \P(A)$ is
itself the **law of large numbers**, whose quantitative cousin, Chebyshev's inequality,
appears on Day 5.)

</Detail>
</Beat>

<Beat>
<Summary>

Examples — fair vs biased coin, fair vs loaded die: same $\Omega$ but a different $\P$ changes the answer (e.g. "even" is $1/2$ vs $7/10$).

</Summary>
<Detail>

<CoinBuilder client:visible />

- **Fair coin.** $\P(\texttt{H}) = \P(\texttt{T}) = 1/2$.
- **Biased coin.** Same $\Omega$, but $\P(\texttt{H}) = 2/3$, $\P(\texttt{T}) = 1/3$ — so $\P$ is its own ingredient, separate from $\Omega$.
- **Fair die.** $\P(k) = 1/6$; for $A = \set{2,4,6}$, $\P(A) = \tfrac12$.
- **Loaded die.** $\P(6) = 1/2$, others $1/10$. The *same* event $\set{2,4,6}$ now has $\P(A) = \tfrac1{10}+\tfrac1{10}+\tfrac12 = \tfrac{7}{10}$ — same event, different measure, different answer.
- **Degenerate.** On $\set{a,b,c}$, $\P(a)=1$, $\P(b)=\P(c)=0$ is legal but boring.

</Detail>
</Beat>

<Beat>
<Summary>

Elementary properties — $\P(\emptyset)=0$, $\P(A^c)=1-\P(A)$, monotonicity, inclusion–exclusion, and the **union bound** $\P(A \cup B) \le \P(A)+\P(B)$.

</Summary>
<Detail>

> For events $A, B \subseteq \Omega$:
> 1. $\P(\emptyset) = 0$;
> 2. $\P(A^c) = 1 - \P(A)$;
> 3. if $A \subseteq B$ then $\P(A) \le \P(B)$;
> 4. $\P(A \cup B) = \P(A) + \P(B) - \P(A \cap B)$;
> 5. $\P(A \cup B) \le \P(A) + \P(B)$ — the **union bound**.

*Why:* (1) $\emptyset$ is disjoint from itself, so $\P(\emptyset) = 2\P(\emptyset)$. (2)
$A$ and $A^c$ are disjoint with union $\Omega$. (3) $B = A \cup (B \setminus A)$ is a
disjoint union. (4) Split $A \cup B$ and $B$ into disjoint pieces and add. (5) Follows
from (4) since $\P(A \cap B) \ge 0$. The union bound extends to any number of events and
is a workhorse of the workshop.

</Detail>
</Beat>

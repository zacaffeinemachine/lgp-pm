Independence is the rule that turns "$A$ and $B$" into a product — used in that form,
without philosophy.

<Lecture>

<Beat>
<Summary>

**Definition** — independence: $\P(A \cap B) = \P(A)\,\P(B)$.

</Summary>
<Detail>

> Events $A$ and $B$ are **independent** if $\P(A \cap B) = \P(A) \cdot \P(B)$.

Intuitively, knowing whether $A$ occurred does not change the probability of $B$. More
generally, $A_1, \dots, A_n$ are **mutually independent** if for *every* subset of
indices,

$$
\P(A_{i_1} \cap \cdots \cap A_{i_k}) = \P(A_{i_1}) \cdots \P(A_{i_k}).
$$

One must check the rule for every subset, not only for pairs.

</Detail>
</Beat>

<Beat>
<Summary>

Intuition misleads — fixed points of a random permutation: $\P(A_1 \cap A_2) = \tfrac{1}{n(n-1)} > \tfrac{1}{n^2}$, so they are not independent.

</Summary>
<Detail>

Let $\pi$ be a uniformly random permutation of $\set{1, \dots, n}$, and
$A_i = \set{\pi(i) = i}$. These *look* independent but are not. In $\Omega = S_n$,

$$
\P(A_1) = \frac{(n-1)!}{n!} = \frac1n, \qquad
\P(A_1 \cap A_2) = \frac{(n-2)!}{n!} = \frac{1}{n(n-1)}.
$$

If independent, the joint probability would be $1/n^2$; instead
$\tfrac{1}{n(n-1)} > \tfrac{1}{n^2}$. Knowing $\pi(1) = 1$ makes $\pi(2) = 2$ *more*
likely — once the value $1$ is used, the value $2$ has one fewer competitor.

</Detail>

<Beat>
<Summary>

Avoiding many events — $\P\!\left(\bigcap_i A_i^c\right) = \prod_i (1-\P(A_i)) \le e^{-\sum_i \P(A_i)}$: independence gives sharp exponential decay.

</Summary>
<Detail>

To argue "none of a long list of bad things happens", apply the multiplication rule to
the complements:

> If $A_1, \dots, A_n$ are mutually independent, then
> $$\P\!\left(\bigcap_{i=1}^n A_i^c\right) = \prod_{i=1}^n \bigl(1 - \P(A_i)\bigr).$$

(If the $A_i$ are mutually independent, so are their complements.) A consequence we
return to repeatedly: for any reals $x_i$,

$$
\prod_{i=1}^n (1 - x_i) \le \exp\!\left(-\sum_{i=1}^n x_i\right),
$$

since $1 - x \le e^{-x}$. So even when the union bound is too crude, independence can
deliver sharp **exponential** decay.

<div class="exercises">

1. Events $A$ and $B$ are independent with $\P(A) = 1/2$ and $\P(B) = 1/3$. Find $\P(A \cap B)$ and $\P(A \cup B)$.
2. A fair coin is tossed three times, so $\Omega = \set{\texttt{H},\texttt{T}}^3$ with the uniform measure. Let $A$ be "the first toss is `H`" and $B$ be "the second toss is `T`". By counting, decide whether $A$ and $B$ are independent.
3. Suppose $A_1, \dots, A_n$ are mutually independent, each with probability $p$. Write $\P(\text{none of them occurs})$ as a product, and give an upper bound for it using $1 - x \le e^{-x}$.
